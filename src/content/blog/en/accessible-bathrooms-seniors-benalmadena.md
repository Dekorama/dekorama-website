## Key Features of an Accessible Bathroom

Designing an accessible bathroom involves thoughtful consideration of every element to maximise safety and usability. Here are the core features that transform a standard bathroom into an accessible sanctuary.

### Walk-in Showers and Wet Rooms

Eliminating the need to step over a high curb is perhaps the most critical change. Walk-in showers or full wet rooms provide seamless, level entry, making them ideal for individuals using wheelchairs or walkers, or those with limited mobility.

*   **Curbless Entry:** A smooth transition from the bathroom floor to the shower area prevents tripping hazards.
*   **Shower Seats and Benches:** Built-in or foldable shower seats offer a safe place to rest, reducing fatigue and the risk of falls while showering.
*   **Handheld Showerheads:** These are easier to use while seated and provide greater control over water direction.

### Grab Bars and Support Rails

Strategically placed grab bars offer crucial support for balance and movement. They are essential near the toilet, inside the shower, and alongside the bathtub if one is present.

*   **Placement and Installation:** Grab bars must be professionally installed into wall studs or with appropriate anchors to ensure they can bear significant weight. Correct placement is key for maximum effectiveness.

### Non-Slip Flooring

Slippery floors are a leading cause of bathroom accidents. Choosing the right flooring material is vital for safety.

*   **Material Choices:** [Porcelain tiles in Málaga](/en/porcelanicos-malaga) with a high slip resistance rating are an excellent choice. Textured finishes, matte surfaces, and smaller tiles with more grout lines can also enhance traction.

### Comfort-Height Toilets

These toilets are taller than standard models, making sitting down and standing up much easier and less strenuous. They are typically around 43-48 cm (17-19 inches) from the floor to the top of the seat.

### Accessible Sinks and Vanities

Sink areas should be designed for easy access, whether standing or seated.

*   **Wall-Mounted Sinks:** These allow wheelchair users to roll underneath comfortably. Ensure there is clear knee space.
*   **Lever Faucets:** Single-lever taps are much easier to operate for individuals with limited hand dexterity compared to traditional knob-style taps.

### Wider Doorways

For wheelchair access, doorways should ideally be a minimum of 80 cm (32 inches) wide. If space allows, 90 cm (36 inches) provides even greater ease of movement.

### Adequate Lighting

Bright, even lighting throughout the bathroom reduces shadows and improves visibility, which is crucial for preventing missteps and enhancing overall safety.
